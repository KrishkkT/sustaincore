Why it matters

SASB speaks the language
institutional investors use.

ESG rating agencies, institutional investors and activist shareholders use SASB metrics to compare companies within sectors. If your peers disclose SASB and you do not, you are invisible in their models.

ISSB Alignment
ISSB IFRS S1 and S2 are built on SASB foundations. Companies that implement SASB today are substantially ready for IFRS S2 mandatory climate disclosure and IFRS S1 general sustainability disclosure.
Investor Valuation Models
Major institutional investors and ESG rating agencies (MSCI, Sustainalytics, S&P ESG) use SASB-aligned metrics as inputs into valuation and risk adjustment models. Missing data is scored as zero.
Comparability Advantage
SASB’s industry-specific approach ensures you are disclosing the metrics that actually matter for your sector — not generic ESG data — enabling like-for-like comparison that investors require.
What we do

SASB implementation —
from classification to disclosure

We identify your applicable SASB standards, collect financially material ESG data and integrate disclosures seamlessly with your existing reporting.

SICS Industry Classification
We identify your applicable SASB Sustainable Industry Classification System (SICS) sector and the specific industry standards that apply — which defines exactly which metrics matter to investors in your space.
Financially Material Metric Identification
From the applicable standard, we identify the quantitative metrics required, their SASB-defined boundaries and measurement units, and map them to your internal data sources.
Data Collection & Calculation
We collect and calculate all required SASB metrics to SASB definition standards — using primary data wherever available and documented estimation methodologies where primary data is unavailable.
SASB-GRI-TCFD Integration
SASB, GRI and TCFD share significant data overlap. We integrate your SASB data collection into a single architecture that eliminates duplication and keeps all disclosures internally consistent.
Investor Communication Integration
SASB disclosures are formatted for integration into Annual Reports, CDP questionnaire responses, ESG data providers (Bloomberg, Refinitiv) and investor relations materials.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

The SASB Sustainable Industry Classification System (SICS) organises companies into 77 industries across 11 sectors based on their resource intensity, environmental impact profile and social risk exposure — rather than traditional financial sector classifications like GICS or SIC. Many large companies span multiple SICS industries. We conduct a detailed business model analysis to determine which SICS classification(s) apply and which specific SASB standards — and the metrics within them — are financially material for your investor-facing disclosure.

SASB standards form the primary technical basis for ISSB IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) and contribute significantly to IFRS S1 (General Sustainability-related Disclosures). ISSB’s transition guidance explicitly recommends using SASB standards as implementation guidance for IFRS S1 sector-specific requirements. Companies that have implemented SASB are therefore substantially ahead in their IFRS readiness — requiring incremental additions rather than a fundamental disclosure redesign.

SASB, GRI and BRSR serve different purposes and are complementary rather than alternatives. GRI is impact-focused (how your company affects the environment and society), BRSR is an Indian regulatory requirement, and SASB is investor-focused (how environmental and social issues affect your company’s financial performance). Companies with significant institutional investor exposure — listed companies, companies with PE backing or international supply chain relationships — benefit from SASB disclosure because it speaks directly to investor valuation models in a way GRI alone does not.
