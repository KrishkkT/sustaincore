Why it matters

Battery EPR is not optional.
It is a statutory obligation.

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 created a sweeping compliance framework for anyone who manufactures, imports or sells batteries in India. Here is what is at stake.

01
EPR Registration is Mandatory
Every producer, importer and refurbisher of batteries — regardless of scale — must register on the CPCB EPR portal before placing batteries in the market. Operating without registration is a direct violation of the Rules and triggers enforcement action.
02
Annual Collection Targets
Registered producers must meet annually escalating collection and recycling targets for each battery category. Failure to meet targets requires purchase of EPR certificates from registered recyclers — missing even one year creates cumulative liability.
03
Import Clearance Risk
Importers of batteries who are not EPR-registered face detention of consignments at ports. Customs authorities are increasingly coordinating with CPCB to verify EPR compliance before granting clearance, making non-registration a direct business disruption risk.
04
Strict Liability Penalties
The Environment Protection Act provides for penalties up to ₹1 lakh per day of default plus criminal prosecution for responsible officers. Battery waste violations are treated as environmental offences, not merely administrative lapses.

Frequently asked questions

Yes. The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 define "producer" broadly to include manufacturers of battery-integrated appliances and devices. If your product contains a battery — whether a laptop, power tool, electric vehicle or UPS — you are a producer under the Rules and required to register on the CPCB EPR portal. The obligation attaches to the battery inside the product, not only to standalone battery sellers.

EPR targets are computed as a percentage of batteries placed in the market in a base year. The percentage escalates annually — starting at 30% in FY 2023-24 and rising to 70% by FY 2027-28 for most battery categories. Targets are separately computed for each of the four categories: portable, automotive, industrial and EV batteries. We compute your exact target based on your sales/import declarations and maintain the evidence chain to demonstrate target achievement.

Yes — the Rules permit producers to fulfil targets by purchasing EPR certificates from CPCB-registered recyclers who have collected and processed batteries. This is the practical route for most producers who do not have a direct consumer collection network. We identify verified recyclers, negotiate certificate procurement agreements, and ensure the certificates uploaded on the CPCB portal match your declared targets — avoiding shortfall penalties.

Yes. All battery chemistries are covered — lead-acid, lithium-ion, nickel-cadmium, nickel-metal hydride, and others. Lead-acid batteries fall under the "automotive" or "industrial" category depending on application. Previously, lead-acid batteries were separately regulated under the Batteries (Management and Handling) Rules, 2001, which have now been superseded by the 2022 Rules. Companies that had registrations under the older rules must re-register under the new framework.

With complete documentation, CPCB EPR portal registration is typically processed in 15–30 working days. The most common delays arise from incomplete battery category declarations, insufficient supporting documents (product catalogues, import data, GST returns) and portal technical issues. We prepare the application with complete documentation upfront, reducing the likelihood of query-driven delays.
