Why it matters

Ambient monitoring is required at
every regulatory checkpoint.

Environmental Clearances, half-yearly compliance reports, EIA studies and GPCB inspections all require valid ambient air quality data. Without it, your regulatory submissions are incomplete and rejectable.

01
EIA Baseline Requirement
Every Environmental Impact Assessment study requires a minimum one-season (preferably two-season) baseline ambient air quality dataset from the study area — covering a 5–10 km radius around the proposed project. This baseline is mandatory for MoEF&CC / SEIAA EC application and TOR (Terms of Reference) compliance. Without it, your EIA report is technically deficient and will be rejected at scoping.
02
EC Compliance Half-Yearly Report
Industries holding Environmental Clearances under the EIA Notification are required to submit half-yearly compliance reports to the MoEF&CC and SEIAA. These reports must include current ambient air quality monitoring data from the prescribed monitoring locations around the facility — typically the fence line, leeward side, nearest habitation and a background location.
03
GPCB Consent Conditions
GPCB Consent to Operate conditions for Red and Orange category industries frequently require periodic ambient air quality monitoring at specified boundary locations, submitted as part of the annual or bi-annual consent renewal package. Gaps in monitoring data are treated as consent violations during GPCB inspection, with consequences for consent renewal and category classification.
04
Dispersion Modelling Validation
Air dispersion modelling — required in EIA studies to predict ground-level concentrations from stack emissions — must be validated against measured ambient air quality data. Without measured baseline and downwind data, the modelling is unvalidated and vulnerable to challenge during public consultation and EAC review hearings.
What we do

From site selection to
regulatory submission

We design, conduct and report ambient air quality monitoring programmes covering EIA baseline studies, EC compliance monitoring, seasonal surveys and continuous AAQMS deployments — all producing NABL-accredited results in CPCB/GPCB-prescribed formats.

Monitoring Location Selection & Network Design
CPCB guideline-compliant monitoring location selection considering prevailing wind direction, sensitive receptors (hospitals, schools, residential areas), topography and EC/GPCB prescribed locations. We optimise the network to provide maximum regulatory utility from minimum station count.
Hi-Volume Sampling Campaigns
24-hour Hi-Vol sampling for PM₁₀, PM₂.₅, SO₂, NO₂, CO and additional parameters (Pb, HF, HCl, NH₃, VOCs as applicable) at all designated locations. Minimum 8 consecutive 24-hour sampling days per season per CPCB protocol for EIA baseline studies.
NABL-Accredited Laboratory Analysis
All collected filter samples analysed at our NABL-accredited laboratory using gravimetric and wet chemical methods prescribed in IS 5182 and CPCB technical guidelines. Full QA/QC including field blanks, duplicates and spike recovery documentation.
Meteorological Data Collection
Simultaneous measurement of wind speed, wind direction, temperature, relative humidity and atmospheric stability class at each monitoring location — essential for air dispersion modelling validation and regulatory reporting.
Regulatory Report Preparation & Submission
Complete ambient air quality monitoring report in CPCB/GPCB/MoEF prescribed format: all monitoring results vs. NAAQ Standards, wind roses, meteorological summaries, NABL test reports and station location maps. Ready for direct insertion into EIA chapters or EC compliance reports.

FAQ

Frequently asked
questions

CPCB guidelines for EIA studies typically require a minimum of 4 monitoring locations within the 5 km study area: one upwind (background), one leeward (downwind, most impacted direction), one nearest sensitive receptor (school, hospital, habitation) and one fence line location. The minimum monitoring duration is 8 consecutive 24-hour sampling days per season per location. Most EIA TOR conditions require two-season baseline (typically pre-monsoon and post-monsoon), making the total dataset 16 sampling days per location across two seasons. The number of locations can increase for larger study areas or projects with multiple emission sources — we advise on the optimal network during the scoping phase.

The parameters required for EC compliance ambient monitoring are specified in your specific Environmental Clearance conditions and vary by industry category. The minimum standard set for most industries includes PM₁₀, PM₂.₅, SO₂ and NO₂. Industries with specific additional pollutant risks (e.g., cement: fluoride; chemicals: HCl, NH₃, specific VOCs; lead smelters: Pb) will have additional parameters listed in their EC. We review your EC conditions at kickoff to confirm the exact parameter list, ensure all are covered and identify any parameters requiring specialised analytical methods not available in standard Hi-Vol sampling — which require additional planning.

Continuous Ambient Air Quality Monitoring Stations (AAQMS) are increasingly accepted by CPCB and some SPCBs as an alternative or supplement to periodic Hi-Vol sampling for EC compliance reporting — particularly for PM₁₀, PM₂.₅, SO₂, NOₓ, O₃ and CO. However, they cannot replace NABL-accredited Hi-Vol analysis for all parameters specified in an EC condition, and some parameters (heavy metals, fluoride, specific VOCs) still require Hi-Vol filter collection and laboratory analysis. We advise on the most efficient combination of AAQMS continuous monitoring and targeted periodic Hi-Vol sampling to satisfy all EC conditions at minimum total cost.

Yes — provided the monitoring network, parameters, sampling duration and NABL accreditation meet the requirements of both purposes. EIA baseline studies and GPCB compliance monitoring share the same NAAQ Standard benchmarks and CPCB sampling protocols. We design monitoring programmes to simultaneously satisfy EIA TOR conditions, EC compliance requirements and GPCB consent conditions wherever possible — avoiding the cost and complexity of running separate parallel programmes. The key is confirming the specific locations and parameters required by each authority before the sampling campaign begins, so that a single well-designed campaign generates regulatory-grade data acceptable to all.
