Why it matters

Risks of groundwater contamination

Groundwater contamination carries severe consequences — legal, financial, operational, and reputational.

01

NGT Liability

The National Green Tribunal has imposed environmental compensation exceeding ₹100 crore for groundwater contamination cases. NGT orders have mandated facility closure, remediation at company cost, and compensation to affected communities.

02

CGWA Compliance

Central Ground Water Authority NOC conditions require periodic groundwater monitoring and reporting. Failure to comply can result in NOC revocation, extraction restrictions, and penalty for illegal groundwater use.

03

Community Conflict

Groundwater contamination affecting drinking water sources creates intense community opposition. Contamination allegations — even unproven — damage reputation, invite regulatory scrutiny, and threaten social license to operate.

04

Remediation Costs

Groundwater remediation is technically complex and extremely expensive — often running into crores for contaminated aquifers. Prevention through monitoring is far more cost-effective than remediation after contamination occurs.

05

Due Diligence Risk

M&A due diligence and buyer environmental assessments examine groundwater monitoring records. Gaps in monitoring or evidence of contamination can derail transactions, reduce valuations, or require extensive indemnities.

06

EC Compliance

Environmental Clearance conditions typically mandate groundwater monitoring around the facility. Half-yearly compliance reports require submission of monitoring data — non-compliance can jeopardise EC validity.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Monitoring frequency depends on regulatory requirements and site risk profile. CGWA NOC and EC conditions typically specify quarterly or half-yearly monitoring. For high-risk facilities (hazardous waste storage, chemical manufacturing) or sites with known contamination concerns, monthly monitoring may be appropriate. Seasonal monitoring (pre-monsoon, post-monsoon) captures aquifer recharge effects. We recommend at minimum quarterly monitoring for most industrial facilities.

Monitoring network design depends on facility size, potential contamination sources, and hydrogeology. At minimum, you need one upgradient (background) well and two or more downgradient wells to detect contamination migration. Additional wells may be needed around specific sources (hazardous waste storage, ETP, chemical storage). CGWA or EC conditions may specify minimum requirements. We conduct hydrogeological assessment to design an adequate network.

Existing borewells can be used if they meet certain criteria — known construction details, appropriate depth and screen interval for the target aquifer, and suitable location relative to contamination sources. Production wells actively used for extraction are not ideal for monitoring due to mixing of water from different depths. Purpose-built monitoring wells with known construction provide more reliable data. We assess existing wells and recommend whether dedicated monitoring wells are needed.

If monitoring detects contamination above drinking water standards, we immediately notify you and conduct preliminary source assessment. Depending on severity, recommended actions may include: increased monitoring frequency, expanded monitoring network, source investigation, interim containment measures, or formal contamination assessment and remediation planning. Early detection through regular monitoring enables proactive response before contamination spreads or triggers regulatory action.

CDP Water questionnaire asks about water quality impacts and groundwater management practices. Groundwater monitoring data demonstrates responsible stewardship and provides evidence for responses about water quality management, pollution prevention, and environmental impact monitoring. Organisations with documented groundwater monitoring programmes score higher on water stewardship criteria. The trend data also supports target-setting for water quality improvement.
