Why it matters

Non-functional CEMS means
automatic non-compliance.

The GPCB monitors data transmission in real time. Gaps in data, sensor failures or CEMS downtime of more than 10% in any month automatically trigger non-compliance flags — regardless of actual emission levels.

Automatic Non-Compliance Flagging
The GPCB€™s central server monitors data continuity in real time. Any gap exceeding 15 minutes, data transmission failure, or sensor malfunction is automatically flagged. Industries with more than 10% data unavailability in a month are listed as non-compliant — without any inspector visit.
Consent to Operate Linkage
Functional CEMS with active data transmission is a mandatory condition in the CTO of all mandated industries. An inoperative or non-transmitting CEMS is treated as a violation of CTO conditions and can result in notice of consent revocation, irrespective of whether actual emissions are within limits.
Real-Time Exceedance Detection
When CEMS data exceeds prescribed limits for two or more consecutive 15-minute averaging periods, the GPCB server automatically generates an exceedance alert. This triggers regulatory correspondence and, in severe cases, immediate show-cause action without waiting for periodic manual monitoring reports.

CEMS is mandated by GPCB for 17 major industry categories under its 2014 Direction and subsequent amendments. The mandated categories include: thermal power plants, cement plants, integrated iron and steel, petroleum refineries, fertiliser plants, pulp and paper mills, sugar industries, distilleries, chlor-alkali plants, caustic soda plants, common effluent treatment plants (CETPs), common biomedical waste treatment facilities, common hazardous waste incineration and treatment facilities, glass manufacturing, tanneries, zinc and lead smelters, and copper smelters. The specific stacks, parameters and transmission requirements vary by sector. You should also check whether your GPCB or individual CTO specifies CEMS requirements beyond the national mandate, as several states have expanded the list.

The GPCB server automatically records data gaps. If transmission is interrupted for more than 15 minutes, the gap is logged. If total data unavailability exceeds 10% in any calendar month, the industry is automatically classified as non-compliant for that month — regardless of actual emission levels. Persistent non-compliance triggers a show-cause notice. These are three quality assurance levels defined under European Standard EN 14181, which the GPCB has adopted as the reference for CEMS quality assurance in India. QAL1 is the type-approval certification of the analyser equipment itself — demonstrated by the manufacturer before sale. Only QAL1-certified analysers are accepted for GPCB-mandated CEMS. QAL2 is the site-specific calibration of a QAL1-certified analyser installed at your particular stack — conducted after installation using parallel reference method measurements. It establishes site-specific calibration functions accounting for your gas matrix. AST (Annual Surveillance Test) is a repeat parallel reference method test conducted annually to confirm that the QAL2 calibration remains valid. All three are required for a GPCB-compliant CEMS installation.
