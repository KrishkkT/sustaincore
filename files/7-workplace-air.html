Workplace Air
Quality Monitoring
OEL · TWA · STEL Assessment

Workplace air quality monitoring measures the concentration of airborne chemical, dust and biological hazards at worker breathing zones inside your facility — comparing them against Occupational Exposure Limits (OELs). It is a legal duty under the Factories Act, 1948, the Manufacture, Storage and Import of Hazardous Chemicals Rules and ISO 45001. It also directly drives your EcoVadis Labour score and BRSR Principle 5 disclosures on worker health and safety.

Monitoring zone types

Where we sample —
personal, area and source zones

A comprehensive workplace air quality survey covers three distinct sampling zones, each answering a different question about worker exposure and emission source control.

Zone A
Personal Breathing Zone
Lapel or helmet-mounted personal air sampler worn by the worker for the full 8-hour shift. Provides the most legally defensible measure of individual exposure. Required for ACGIH TLV/TWA comparison and chronic health risk assessment.
Zone B
Area / General Workplace
Fixed samplers placed at representative locations across the work area at breathing height (1.5m). Identifies spatial distribution of contamination and areas requiring engineering controls or improved ventilation.
Zone C
Source / Near-Process Zone
Samplers positioned close to specific emission sources — open tanks, mixing operations, welding points, furnace openings — to characterise source strength for local exhaust ventilation design.
Zone D
Control Room / Office
Background and control zone measurements confirming that engineering barriers are effective and that administrative and clerical workers near production areas are not at risk of secondary exposure.
Why it matters

Worker exposure is a legal,
ethical and commercial obligation.

Unmonitored workplace air quality exposes your business to criminal liability, insurance exclusions, occupational disease compensation claims, ISO 45001 non-conformances and EcoVadis score penalties — simultaneously.

01
Factories Act Legal Duty
Under Sections 11–20 of the Factories Act, 1948, employers have a statutory duty to maintain workplace environments free from hazardous concentrations of dust, fumes and chemicals. Factory Inspectors have powers to enter, inspect and prosecute. Failure to monitor is itself an offence, independent of whether actual exposures exceed limits.
02
ISO 45001 & OHSAS 18001
ISO 45001 Clause 6.1 requires hazard identification and occupational health risk assessment as a core management system requirement. Workplace air quality monitoring data is the primary evidence base for chemical exposure risk assessment. Without it, an ISO 45001 audit will raise a major non-conformance.
03
EcoVadis & BRSR Scoring
EcoVadis evaluates occupational health and safety management as part of its Labour & Human Rights theme — one of the four weighted scoring criteria. Documented workplace air quality monitoring with corrective action programmes significantly improves your Labour theme score. BRSR Principle 5 (P5) requires specific KPI disclosure on workplace health and safety, including monitoring data.
04
Occupational Disease Liability
Chronic occupational diseases — silicosis, asbestosis, chronic solvent encephalopathy, noise-induced hearing loss — attract workmen’s compensation claims, insurance litigation and reputational damage. Regular workplace monitoring, documented corrective actions and engineering controls are your primary legal defence against occupational disease compensation claims.
What we do

From hazard survey to
exposure report & corrective plan

We conduct complete industrial hygiene surveys: hazard identification, sampling strategy design, personal and area air sampling, NABL-accredited laboratory analysis and a full occupational exposure assessment report with risk-ranked corrective recommendations.

Workplace Hazard Walk-Through Survey
Industrial hygienist-led facility walk-through to identify all chemical, dust and biological hazards, assess exposure routes, evaluate existing controls and design a targeted sampling strategy covering the most material exposure scenarios.
Personal Air Sampling (TWA & STEL)
Full-shift (8-hour) personal air sampling at the breathing zone of workers in high-exposure job categories, using calibrated low-flow pumps and appropriate sample media (XAD, OVS, silica gel, PTFE filters). Short-term samples for STEL assessment during peak-exposure tasks.
Area Air Sampling
Fixed-location samplers across all production zones, storage areas, loading bays and control rooms — mapping the spatial distribution of contamination and identifying localised hotspots requiring priority engineering intervention.
NABL-Accredited Laboratory Analysis
Collected samples analysed by our NABL-accredited laboratory using NIOSH, OSHA and IS 5182 analytical methods for each target analyte — producing quantified results with analytical uncertainty, detection limits and QA/QC documentation.
Occupational Exposure Report & Risk Assessment
Full report comparing all measured concentrations against applicable OELs (TWA and STEL), risk-ranking each exposure scenario by severity, identifying which workers are at chronic or acute risk, and providing prioritised engineering, administrative and PPE recommendations.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

The Time-Weighted Average (TWA) is the 8-hour average airborne concentration to which a worker may be repeatedly exposed day after day without adverse health effects — the chronic exposure limit. The Short-Term Exposure Limit (STEL) is the 15-minute average concentration that should not be exceeded at any time during the working day, even if the 8-hour TWA is within limits — it protects against acute effects from brief high-concentration exposures. Both limits are set by ACGIH as Threshold Limit Values (TLVs), and where Indian standards exist under the Factories Act Schedules, those apply. We measure both — personal full-shift sampling for TWA and short-duration grab or pump samples during peak-exposure tasks for STEL.

Under ISO 45001 and good industrial hygiene practice, monitoring frequency depends on exposure risk level. Initial baseline monitoring should be conducted when any new chemical is introduced or processes change significantly. For ongoing surveillance: exposures below 0.1× OEL — annually or on process change; exposures 0.1–0.5× OEL — every 6 months; exposures 0.5–1× OEL — quarterly; exposures above OEL — monthly until corrective actions reduce concentrations below the limit. The Factories Act and specific industry rules (chemical, hazardous chemicals rules) may prescribe more frequent monitoring for specific substances or factory categories.

Occupational noise dosimetry and heat stress measurement (WBGT — Wet Bulb Globe Temperature) are separate monitoring services from airborne chemical and dust monitoring, but we provide all three as part of a comprehensive industrial hygiene survey. Noise dosimetry measures workers’ daily noise exposure against the 85 dB(A) action level and 90 dB(A) limit under the Factories Act. WBGT heat stress monitoring is critical for workers in foundries, glass plants, steel mills and outdoor operations. We recommend combining air quality, noise and heat stress monitoring into a single facility survey to minimise production disruption and reduce total assessment cost.
