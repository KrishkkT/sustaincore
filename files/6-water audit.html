Understanding water audits

What a water audit reveals

A water audit systematically tracks water from source to discharge — quantifying inputs, uses, losses, and outputs. This water balance is the foundation for identifying conservation opportunities and prioritising investments.

Why it matters

Business case for water conservation

Water conservation delivers direct cost savings while addressing regulatory, operational, and reputational risks.

Cost Reduction

Water costs include procurement (municipal supply, tankers, groundwater extraction), treatment (raw water treatment, ETP operation), and disposal (CETP charges, ZLD costs). Reducing consumption cuts costs across all three — typically 15-30% savings are achievable.

Supply Security

Groundwater levels are declining across industrial belts. Municipal supplies are increasingly unreliable. Reducing water dependence through conservation and recycling insulates your operations from supply disruptions that can halt production.

Regulatory Compliance

CGWA and SGWA groundwater extraction limits, GPCB water consumption conditions, and ZLD mandates create regulatory constraints on water use. Conservation reduces regulatory exposure and supports consent compliance.

ESG Performance

Water is a material ESG issue. BRSR requires water consumption reporting. CDP Water assesses water stewardship. Global buyers evaluate supplier water practices. A documented conservation programme strengthens your sustainability credentials.

Effluent Reduction

Less water in means less wastewater out. Water conservation directly reduces ETP load, effluent treatment costs, and discharge volumes — making ZLD compliance more achievable and cost-effective.

Community Relations

Industrial water use is increasingly contentious in water-stressed regions. Demonstrable conservation efforts reduce community opposition, support social license to operate, and mitigate reputational risks associated with water conflicts.

Comprehensive water audit services

Water Source Assessment

Mapping all water sources — groundwater, municipal supply, surface water, rainwater, recycled water. Quantifying extraction volumes, costs, quality, and reliability for each source.

Conservation Plan Development

Conservation opportunities

Typical savings areas

Water audits consistently identify conservation opportunities across these categories.

Leaks

Leak Detection & Repair

Underground leaks, valve failures, fitting issues, and distribution system losses often account for 5-15% of water consumption. Systematic detection and repair provides immediate savings.

Cooling

Cooling System Optimisation

Cooling towers are major water consumers. Increasing cycles of concentration, reducing blowdown, optimising makeup water, and implementing dry cooling where feasible can reduce cooling water use by 20-40%.

Process

Process Optimisation

Counter-current washing, cascade rinsing, trigger sprays, process modifications, and equipment upgrades can significantly reduce process water consumption while maintaining product quality.

Recycle

Recycling & Reuse

Treated effluent reuse, condensate recovery, cooling tower blowdown reuse, and rainwater harvesting can substitute 30-50% of freshwater consumption in many industries.

Utility

Utility Optimisation

Boiler blowdown heat recovery, condensate return, steam trap maintenance, and domestic water efficiency (low-flow fixtures, sensor taps) reduce utility water consumption.

Monitor

Metering & Monitoring

What gets measured gets managed. Sub-metering key consumption points, real-time monitoring, and water accounting systems enable ongoing optimisation and rapid leak detection.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

A comprehensive water audit typically takes 4-6 weeks from kickoff to final report. This includes preliminary data collection (1 week), site assessment and measurements (1-2 weeks including multiple visits to capture operational variations), analysis and water balance development (1 week), and report preparation with conservation plan (1-2 weeks). Complex multi-facility audits may require longer timelines.

Most facilities achieve 15-30% water reduction through implementation of audit recommendations. Initial savings come from no-cost and low-cost measures — leak repairs, operational improvements, behavioural changes. Larger savings require capital investment in recycling systems, process modifications, or equipment upgrades. We provide ROI analysis for each measure so you can prioritise investments based on payback.

Sub-metering improves audit accuracy but is not a prerequisite. We can conduct audits using a combination of existing meters, temporary flow measurements, runtime calculations, and engineering estimates. The audit often recommends priority sub-metering points as a first step for ongoing water management — so metering infrastructure recommendations are included in our conservation plan.

CDP Water questionnaire requires detailed water accounting — withdrawals by source, consumption, discharge by destination, and water recycling. A water audit generates exactly this data. Additionally, CDP asks about water management practices, targets, and governance — the conservation plan provides evidence for these responses. Organisations with documented water audits and conservation programmes score significantly higher on CDP Water.
