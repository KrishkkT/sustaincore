Used Oil Authorization

Used and waste oil is classified as hazardous waste under the Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules, 2016. Any industrial facility, power plant or automotive workshop generating, storing or disposing of used oil requires GPCB authorization — and unauthorized disposal is a criminal offence.

Why it matters

Used oil is hazardous waste.
Unauthorized disposal is criminal.

Every industrial facility that uses lubricating oil, transformer oil, hydraulic fluid or cutting oil generates used oil — classified as hazardous waste under Schedule I of the HW Rules. Understanding your obligations is the first step.

No Quantity Threshold
Unlike some environmental regulations, the Hazardous Waste Rules do not prescribe a minimum generation quantity for authorization. Any unit generating used oil — even a small automotive workshop — requires GPCB authorization to lawfully store and dispose of it.
Disposal to Authorized Entities Only
Used oil may only be transferred to GPCB-registered recyclers, re-refiners or co-processors. Selling to unregistered kabadiwallas, draining into sewers or burning openly are all violations — regardless of how the oil was historically disposed of at your facility.
Storage Compliance Requirements
Used oil must be stored in designated, properly labelled containers or tanks in a covered, impermeable storage area with spill containment. GPCB inspectors routinely examine storage areas during site visits, and inadequate storage is a common violation trigger.
Manifest System Compliance
Every movement of used oil from your facility must be accompanied by a Hazardous Waste Manifest — a tracking document that links generator, transporter and recycler. Incomplete or missing manifests are a direct compliance violation and are checked during GPCB inspections.

Frequently asked questions

The Rules define authorization as applicable to any occupier who handles hazardous waste, and used/waste oil is explicitly listed under Schedule I. No. Used oil may only be transferred to entities that hold a valid GPCB authorization as a used oil recycler or re-refiner. Transferring to unregistered scrap dealers or kabadiwallas — even if paid — makes the generator liable for unauthorized disposal. We verify recycler registration status before linkage and ensure all transfers are accompanied by Hazardous Waste Manifests as required by the Rules.

Schedule I of the HW Rules covers all mineral-based used and waste oils — including lubricating oils, engine oils, hydraulic fluids, gear oils, transformer oils, cutting and quenching oils, and heat transfer fluids. The key criterion is that the oil has been used in industrial or automotive applications and has become unsuitable for its original purpose. Unused oil that is off-specification or contaminated is also covered. Vegetable-based oils follow a different regulatory pathway.

The Manifest is a multi-copy tracking document required for every movement of hazardous waste off your premises. The generator fills and signs the manifest, the transporter endorses it, and the recycler signs the receipt copy — which is then returned to the generator within 90 days. GPCB may ask for the manifest register at any time. Missing, incomplete or unreturned manifests are common violations found during GPCB inspections. We set up the manifest register and train your team to maintain it correctly.

Unauthorized storage or disposal of hazardous waste is an offence under Section 5 of the Environment Protection Act, 1986 — carrying penalties of up to ₹1 lakh per day plus criminal prosecution of responsible officers. GPCB also has the authority to issue a direction to stop operations under Section 5 of the EPA and may initiate prosecution. If your facility has received a GPCB notice or is planning for an upcoming inspection, we prioritize authorization as an emergency filing to demonstrate proactive compliance.
